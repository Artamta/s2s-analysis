\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Subseasonal-to-seasonal (S2S) forecasts at lead times of two to six
weeks support planning decisions in agriculture, water resources, and
disaster preparedness, yet precipitation skill at these leads remains
limited, particularly over tropical and subtropical land regions.
Machine-learning (ML) forecast systems trained on reanalysis data now
match or exceed operational numerical weather prediction at medium
range, but their subseasonal precipitation skill over regional domains
such as India is largely undocumented, especially in a real-time
setting. Here we benchmark two AI-based S2S systems---Spire AI-S2S and
FuXi-S2S---against two operational dynamical systems---the ECMWF
extended-range ensemble and the NCEP Climate Forecast System version~2
(CFSv2)---for weekly precipitation prediction over India during
January--March (JFM) 2026. We evaluate two variables: total
precipitation, the target of greatest user value, and 500-hPa
geopotential height, the large-scale circulation that controls it,
allowing us to ask whether skill for the circulation translates into
skill for precipitation. Ensemble-mean forecasts from 13 weekly
initializations (1~January--26~March~2026) are verified against ERA5 at
weekly leads of one to six weeks over Indian land points, using a
cosine-weighted, spatially centered anomaly pattern correlation (PCC)
for both variables and, additionally, root-mean-square error and mean
bias for geopotential height.

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Subseasonal-to-seasonal (S2S) prediction, covering lead times from
roughly two weeks to two months, bridges the gap between medium-range
weather forecasting and seasonal climate outlooks
\citep{vitart2017,nas2016}. Forecasts at these leads are increasingly
demanded for agricultural planning, reservoir operations, energy load
management, and disaster preparedness \citep{white2017}, yet they
remain among the most difficult products to produce skillfully: the
atmosphere has largely lost its initial-condition memory, while slowly
varying boundary forcings have not yet come to dominate
\citep{mariotti2020}. Documented sources of subseasonal predictability
include the Madden--Julian oscillation \citep[MJO;][]{zhang2005},
stratosphere--troposphere coupling, soil-moisture and snow anomalies,
and the slowly evolving state of the El Ni\~no--Southern Oscillation
(ENSO) \citep{mariotti2020,domeisen2022}. Precipitation, the variable
of greatest practical value to many users, is also the variable for
which S2S skill degrades most rapidly: hindcast-based assessments show
that anomaly correlations over most tropical and subtropical land
regions fall to modest values beyond week~2
\citep{deandrade2019,pegion2019}.

Over India, the January--March (JFM) season---boreal winter
transitioning into early spring---has received far less attention in
the S2S literature than the summer monsoon, despite its societal
importance. Winter precipitation over northern India and the Western
Himalaya is produced chiefly by western disturbances (WDs),
extratropical synoptic-scale systems embedded in the subtropical
westerly jet \citep{dimri2015,hunt2018}. WD-associated precipitation
sustains the rabi (winter) cropping season, including the wheat belt of
the Indo-Gangetic Plain, and builds the Himalayan snowpack that feeds
the Indus and Ganges river systems during the subsequent melt season
\citep{dimri2015}. Southern peninsular India can additionally receive
residual northeast-monsoon rainfall in early January, while anomalously
wet or dry winters modulate fog occurrence, cold-wave conditions, and
pre-monsoon water availability. Skillful multi-week precipitation
outlooks for JFM therefore carry substantial value for Indian
agriculture and water management, and extended-range forecasting for
the region is an operational priority of the India Meteorological
Department (IMD) and the Ministry of Earth Sciences
\citep{rao2019,pattanaik2019}.

In parallel, weather prediction is being reshaped by machine-learning
(ML) forecast systems trained on reanalysis data. Deterministic
data-driven models such as FourCastNet, Pangu-Weather, and GraphCast
now match or exceed operational numerical weather prediction (NWP) on
standard medium-range headline scores
\citep{pathak2022,bi2023,lam2023,benbouallegue2024,rasp2024};
probabilistic ML systems have followed \citep{price2025}; hybrid
approaches couple learned parameterizations to dynamical cores
\citep{kochkov2024}; and operational centers have begun running
data-driven systems alongside their NWP suites \citep{lang2024}.
Extension of these methods to the subseasonal range is more recent:
FuXi-S2S, an ML system producing 42-day ensemble forecasts, has been
reported to outperform conventional S2S models on several global
metrics \citep{chen2024}, and commercial providers now issue AI-based
S2S products \citep{spire2026}. Published evaluations of ML S2S
systems have, however, emphasized global or large-scale
diagnostics---for example, MJO prediction and midlatitude circulation
indices---while regional precipitation skill over densely populated,
hydrologically sensitive domains such as India remains largely
undocumented.

A further gap concerns the evaluation setting. Most S2S skill
estimates derive from multi-year hindcasts
\citep{deandrade2019,pegion2019}, which provide robust statistics but
may not reflect the configuration of a system as actually operated in a
given season---particularly for rapidly evolving AI models. Real-time
(or quasi-real-time) verification of a single season offers a
complementary, if statistically noisier, view: it tests the systems as
users experienced them, with operational initial conditions and
operational model versions. In this study we adopt this real-time
perspective for JFM 2026, benchmarking two AI-based systems---Spire
AI-S2S and FuXi-S2S---against two established operational dynamical
systems---the ECMWF extended-range ensemble and NCEP CFSv2---for weekly
precipitation over India.

Specifically, we address four questions:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item How does weekly skill for precipitation and for the 500-hPa
        circulation over India decay with lead time (weeks 1--6) in
        each system during JFM 2026?
  \item Is SPIRE competitive with the operational dynamical systems
        (ECMWF, NCEP), and is it superior to the other AI system
        (FuXi-S2S), and at which leads?
  \item Does skill for the large-scale circulation (Z500) translate
        into skill for precipitation?
  \item How is skill organized across the IMD homogeneous regions, and
        what systematic biases characterize each system?
\end{enumerate}
We verify ensemble-mean forecasts from 13 weekly initializations
against ERA5 using the anomaly correlation coefficient (ACC),
root-mean-square error (RMSE), and mean bias. The remainder of the
paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:data} describes the
verification data and study domain, Section~\ref{sec:method} the
processing methodology, Section~\ref{sec:models} the forecast systems,
and Section~\ref{sec:verif} the verification framework. Results are
presented in Section~\ref{sec:results} and discussed in
Section~\ref{sec:discussion}; limitations, future work, and conclusions
follow in Sections~\ref{sec:limitations}--\ref{sec:conclusions}.

\section{Data}\label{sec:data}

\subsection{Verification reference: ERA5}\label{sec:era5}

We use the fifth-generation ECMWF reanalysis
\citep[ERA5;][]{hersbach2020} as the verification reference. Hourly
total precipitation (\texttt{tp}) on the native
$0.25^{\circ}\times0.25^{\circ}$ grid is accumulated to daily totals
(00--00~UTC) and subsequently to the weekly windows defined in
Section~\ref{sec:weeks}. ERA5 precipitation is a short-range forecast
product of the IFS rather than a direct observational analysis, and its
quality over the Indian region varies spatially, with the largest
uncertainties over the Western Himalaya, where gauge density is low and
orographic gradients are strong. We therefore treat ERA5 as a
dynamically consistent, temporally complete reference suitable for
anomaly-based verification, and we discuss the implications of this
choice---together with a planned cross-check against the IMD
$0.25^{\circ}$ gauge-based rainfall analysis \citep{pai2014}---in
Sections~\ref{sec:limitations} and \ref{sec:future}.

\section{Methodology}\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Initialization dates and weekly lead windows}\label{sec:weeks}

Thirteen initializations at weekly intervals span the JFM 2026 season:
1, 8, 15, 22, and 29 January; 5, 12, 19, and 26 February; and 5, 12,
19, and 26 March 2026 (all at \ph{00~UTC --- confirm per system}).
Forecast precipitation is aggregated into nonoverlapping weekly
accumulation windows: week~1 corresponds to forecast days 1--7, week~2
to days 8--14, week~3 to days 15--21, and week~4 to days 22--28, where
day~1 denotes the first 24~h after initialization. The verifying
periods collectively span 1~January--\ph{22~April}~2026. \ph{If any
system provides output at a different time resolution or
initialization time (e.g., daily-mean rates rather than accumulations,
or non-00~UTC cycles), document the alignment and conversion here.}
Where forecast horizons permit (Table~\ref{tab:models}), weeks 5--6 may
be added in a future revision.

\subsection{Common grid}\label{sec:grid}

All forecasts and the ERA5 reference are remapped to a common
\ph{1.5$^{\circ}$ (suggested, matching the S2S archive grid;
alternatives: 1.0$^{\circ}$ or 0.25$^{\circ}$)} latitude--longitude
grid prior to verification. First-order conservative remapping is used
for precipitation to preserve area-integrated totals. \ph{If results
are sensitive to the verification grid, report a sensitivity test
(e.g., repeat Table~\ref{tab:acc} on the 0.25$^{\circ}$ grid).}

\subsection{Climatologies and anomaly definition}\label{sec:anom}

Both forecast and observed anomalies are defined by removing a single
common climatology: the ERA5 JFM-2026 seasonal mean field, computed
over the verification period and applied identically to every system
and to the ERA5 reference. Multi-decadal ERA5 climatologies and
system-specific reforecast (hindcast) climatologies were not available
for the real-time configurations evaluated here, so a lead-dependent
model-drift correction could not be applied. Two properties make this
choice defensible for the present comparison. First, the identical
baseline is subtracted from all four systems and from the reference, so
no system is advantaged relative to another. Second, our headline
metric is the spatially \emph{centered} pattern correlation
(Eq.~\ref{eq:acc}), which measures agreement in the \emph{shape} of the
anomaly field and is therefore insensitive to lead-dependent mean
offsets that an in-sample seasonal climatology leaves uncorrected. The
principal residual risk---that an in-sample seasonal mean can modestly
inflate absolute anomaly amplitudes---bears on RMSE rather than on the
pattern correlation, and is carried as a limitation
(Section~\ref{sec:limitations}).

\subsection{Ensemble treatment}\label{sec:ens}

Verification targets the ensemble mean of each system, computed
identically across all four: the SPIRE provider-supplied ensemble mean,
the FuXi-S2S 11-member mean, and the ECMWF and NCEP perturbed-member
(PF) means (100 and 15 members, respectively; member counts in
Table~\ref{tab:models}). Ensemble means from unequal ensemble sizes are
not strictly comparable, because larger ensembles damp unpredictable
variance more effectively; this asymmetry favours the larger ECMWF and
SPIRE ensembles at longer leads and is carried as a caveat
(Section~\ref{sec:limitations}). Probabilistic verification is deferred
to future work (Section~\ref{sec:future}).

\section{Verification Framework}\label{sec:verif}

Let $f'_{i,t,\ell}$ denote the ensemble-mean forecast anomaly at land
grid point $i$, initialization $t$ ($t=1,\dots,T$; $T=13$), and weekly
lead $\ell\in\{1,2,3,4\}$, and let $o'_{i,t,\ell}$ denote the
corresponding ERA5 anomaly for the same verifying week. Area weights
are $w_i=\cos\varphi_i$, with sums taken over the land mask of
Fig.~\ref{fig:domain}.

\subsection{Metrics}\label{sec:metrics}

The anomaly pattern correlation (PCC; we use this term in preference to
the unqualified ``ACC'' to emphasize the spatial centering) at lead
$\ell$ is the cosine-weighted, spatially \emph{centered} Pearson
correlation of the anomaly fields, computed per initialization and
averaged over the season,
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{PCC}(\ell) \;=\; \frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T}
  \frac{\sum_i w_i\,(f'_{i,t,\ell}-\overline{f'}_{t,\ell})\,
                     (o'_{i,t,\ell}-\overline{o'}_{t,\ell})}
       {\sqrt{\sum_i w_i\,(f'_{i,t,\ell}-\overline{f'}_{t,\ell})^{2}}\,
        \sqrt{\sum_i w_i\,(o'_{i,t,\ell}-\overline{o'}_{t,\ell})^{2}}}\,,
  \label{eq:acc}
\end{equation}
where $\overline{x}_{t,\ell}=\big(\sum_i w_i x_{i,t,\ell}\big)/\sum_i w_i$
denotes the cosine-weighted spatial mean over land points. This is the
exact estimator implemented in the verification code; the seasonal
average is taken on the raw per-initialization correlations (not on
Fisher-$z$ transforms). The root-mean-square error is
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{RMSE}(\ell) \;=\; \frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T}
  \sqrt{\frac{\sum_i w_i\,\bigl(f_{i,t,\ell}-o_{i,t,\ell}\bigr)^{2}}
             {\sum_i w_i}}\,,
  \label{eq:rmse}
\end{equation}
evaluated on \ph{full weekly accumulations in mm~week$^{-1}$ (suggested)
and/or on anomalies --- state choice}, and the mean bias is
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{Bias}(\ell) \;=\; \frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T}
  \frac{\sum_i w_i\,\bigl(f_{i,t,\ell}-o_{i,t,\ell}\bigr)}
       {\sum_i w_i}\,,
  \label{eq:bias}
\end{equation}
in the field's native units. \textbf{Variable-specific metric scope.}
The two variables are not verified with the same metric set, for a
concrete data reason. The ERA5 total-precipitation field available for
this study is archived as a single 6-hour accumulation per day (valid
18--00~UTC) rather than a full 24-hour daily total; consequently the
absolute precipitation magnitudes of the reference and of the systems
trained to it differ from those of the systems reporting true daily
totals by an approximately constant multiplicative factor (a factor of
roughly fifteen to twenty in the domain mean). This contaminates
absolute precipitation RMSE and bias, but leaves the spatially centered
pattern correlation (Eq.~\ref{eq:acc}) essentially unaffected, because
that metric is invariant to a uniform rescaling of either field.
\emph{We therefore report precipitation skill using the pattern
correlation only}, and present precipitation bias (Eq.~\ref{eq:bias})
solely as a qualitative wet/dry-tendency diagnostic, explicitly
flagged. Geopotential height, which carries no such accumulation
ambiguity, is verified with the full set: pattern correlation, RMSE,
and mean bias.

For the spatial skill maps (Fig.~\ref{fig:spatial_pcc}), the local
temporal pattern correlation at each grid point is the correlation,
across the $T=13$ initializations, between forecast and observed weekly
Z500 anomalies. With so few samples these maps are necessarily noisy and
are interpreted qualitatively.

\subsection{Reference forecasts}\label{sec:baselines}

Two reference forecasts contextualize all scores: (i)~climatology
(zero anomaly), for which the ACC of Eq.~(\ref{eq:acc}) is undefined
and the anomaly RMSE equals the observed anomaly standard deviation;
and (ii)~persistence, defined as the observed anomaly of the week
immediately preceding initialization, applied at all leads.
The persistence baseline is shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:mme}.

\subsection{Uncertainty estimation}\label{sec:uncertainty}

Confidence intervals for all aggregate scores are estimated by
bootstrap resampling of the 13 initialization dates (\ph{1000}
resamples; percentile \ph{95\%} intervals). Differences between
systems are assessed by paired resampling of score differences
\ph{(describe procedure)}. Given $T=13$, only large differences are
expected to be distinguishable, and all statements of relative skill
in Section~\ref{sec:results} should be read with this caveat.

\subsection{Interpreting deterministic scores for smooth forecasts}
\label{sec:smoothing}

Ensemble means---and, by construction, many ML forecasts---are
smoother than individual realizations of the atmosphere. Deterministic
scores such as RMSE therefore structurally reward low-variance
forecasts (the ``double penalty'' effect;
\citealp{wilks2019,benbouallegue2024}). To make this effect visible we
additionally report \ph{the ratio of forecast-anomaly to
observed-anomaly spatial standard deviation at each lead (suggested;
inexpensive and informative)} and interpret RMSE alongside ACC and
bias rather than in isolation \citep{murphy1988}.

atmospheric circulation over India (Fig.~\ref{fig:acc_lead}b,c). At
week~1 SPIRE attains a Z500 pattern correlation (PCC) of 0.91,
comparable to ECMWF (0.94) and substantially above FuXi-S2S (0.79) and
NCEP (0.80). It remains competitive at longer leads, with PCC of 0.64
and 0.48 at weeks~2 and~3 (versus 0.62 and 0.43 for ECMWF). RMSE tells
the same story (Fig.~\ref{fig:acc_lead}c): SPIRE's week-1 Z500 RMSE of
$\approx$12.0~m is second only to ECMWF (9.3~m) and below FuXi (12.5~m)
and NCEP (19.4~m); at weeks~5--6 SPIRE attains the \emph{lowest} RMSE of
all four systems (31.9~m, versus 35.2, 37.1, and 37.4~m for ECMWF, FuXi,
and NCEP). The paired SPIRE$-$FuXi comparison
(Fig.~\ref{fig:spire_fuxi}b) shows a statistically significant week-1
advantage for SPIRE ($\Delta\mathrm{PCC}={+}0.12$, $p<0.001$). Through
week~3, SPIRE is therefore competitive with the leading operational
system (ECMWF) and outperforms both FuXi and NCEP for circulation
prediction; at weeks~4--6 the four systems become statistically
indistinguishable as all approach low pattern skill (PCC${\lesssim}0.3$).

\subsection{Precipitation skill}\label{sec:precip_skill}

Precipitation pattern skill is far lower than circulation skill for
every system (Fig.~\ref{fig:acc_lead}a). Among the three systems that
produce true daily-total precipitation---SPIRE, ECMWF, and NCEP---week-1
PCC ranges from 0.29 to 0.44 (SPIRE 0.35, ECMWF 0.44, NCEP 0.29) and
falls to near zero by weeks~2--3. Within this group SPIRE is
statistically indistinguishable from the operational systems
(overlapping bootstrap intervals at all leads), so its strong
circulation skill does not carry over into a precipitation advantage.

\paragraph{The FuXi-S2S precipitation result and why it must be read
with caution.} FuXi-S2S records markedly higher precipitation PCC than
every other system---0.82 at week~1, declining only to 0.46 by
week~3---and the paired SPIRE$-$FuXi difference is significantly
negative through week~3 (Fig.~\ref{fig:spire_fuxi}a). We caution
strongly against interpreting this as superior physical precipitation
skill. FuXi-S2S is trained on ERA5 and reproduces precipitation on the
ERA5 native scale (its JFM-2026 domain mean, $\approx$0.03~mm, matches
the 6-hour ERA5 reference, whereas SPIRE, ECMWF, and NCEP produce
true daily totals near 0.8~mm). Because verification is performed
against that same ERA5 field, a system trained to emulate ERA5 is
rewarded for reproducing the spatial structure of the reference itself,
rather than for independently skillful precipitation forecasting. The
three systems that target true daily precipitation cluster together at
much lower PCC, and we regard their range as the physically meaningful
comparison; the FuXi-S2S precipitation advantage is, at least in part,
an artifact of training-target and reference consistency
(Section~\ref{sec:limitations}).

The contrast between high circulation skill and low precipitation skill
(for the true-daily systems) directly answers our third question: useful
predictability of the large-scale circulation extends well beyond the
horizon of useful precipitation predictability over India during
JFM~2026, because precipitation is governed by local and mesoscale
processes that circulation skill alone does not capture.

\subsection{Reference forecasts and the multi-model ensemble}
\label{sec:mme}

Figure~\ref{fig:mme} adds two references to the four systems: a
persistence forecast (the observed weekly anomaly immediately preceding
initialization, applied at all leads) and an equal-weight multi-model
ensemble (MME; the mean of the four systems). For Z500, every system
beats persistence by a wide margin---SPIRE attains PCC of 0.91 at week~1
and remains above persistence at every lead through week~6 (e.g.\ 0.48
versus 0.00 at week~3)---confirming that the circulation skill is
genuine and not a restatement of low-frequency persistence. The MME is
the strongest single product for Z500: its PCC matches the best
individual system at every lead (0.93 at week~1, 0.67 at week~2) while
achieving the lowest RMSE of any forecast, including a week-1 RMSE of
8.1~m below ECMWF's 9.3~m. The PCC gain of the MME over the best single
system is nonetheless small ($|\Delta\mathrm{PCC}|\leq0.04$ at weeks
1--4), so with $T=13$ we frame the MME advantage as a robustness and
error-reduction benefit rather than a large skill gain.

For precipitation the picture is different. The MME precipitation PCC
(0.40 at week~1) tracks the true-daily-total cluster rather than FuXi,
because the equal-weight mean is dominated by the three systems on the
true daily scale. Persistence precipitation PCC (0.37 at week~1, 0.22 at
week~3) is comparable to or higher than that of SPIRE, ECMWF, and NCEP;
however, persistence is computed from ERA5 against ERA5 and therefore
shares the native scale of the reference, exactly as FuXi-S2S does
(Section~\ref{sec:precip_skill}). We therefore do \emph{not} interpret
this as the true-daily systems failing to beat persistence; rather, it
reflects the same reference-consistency effect, and reinforces our
decision to treat precipitation magnitudes cautiously.

A complementary diagnostic is the ratio of forecast to observed
anomaly spatial standard deviation (Section~\ref{sec:smoothing}). For
Z500 this ratio falls for SPIRE from near unity at week~1 to about 0.5
by week~4, indicating that its ensemble mean is progressively smoother
than the observed field, which partly accounts for its low RMSE; FuXi-S2S
shows the opposite, with a ratio rising above 1.4, consistent with its
larger RMSE growth, while ECMWF and NCEP remain near 0.8--1.1. RMSE
differences between systems should be read in this light.

western-disturbance forcing being the dominant source of organized,
predictable winter precipitation over India; FuXi-S2S is positive in all
regions, again reflecting its reference-consistency advantage rather
than physical skill (Section~\ref{sec:precip_skill}). With four regions
and 13 initializations these estimates are indicative rather than
definitive.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

First, how do the JFM 2026 scores compare with the hindcast-based
expectations in the literature? Multi-model S2S hindcast assessments
report useful deterministic precipitation skill over South Asia
typically confined to weeks 1--2, with regime- and
teleconnection-dependent windows of opportunity at longer leads
\citep{deandrade2019,white2017,mariotti2020}. \ph{State whether the
real-time 2026 scores fall within, above, or below those hindcast
ranges, and discuss possible reasons (small sample, season-specific
predictability, system upgrades).}

Second, the deterministic framing structurally favors smooth
forecasts. If the AI systems verify well on RMSE but show reduced
anomaly variance (Section~\ref{sec:smoothing}), part of their
advantage reflects the double-penalty effect rather than superior
placement of precipitation features
\citep{murphy1988,benbouallegue2024,rasp2024}. \ph{Quantify using the
variance ratios and discuss.}

Third, the comparison conflates several factors: ensemble size
(\ph{101} vs.\ \ph{16} vs.\ \ph{N} vs.\ \ph{51} members),
initialization analyses of differing quality, native resolution, and
climatology correction strategies (Section~\ref{sec:anom}).
Differences in scores should therefore be attributed to ``systems as
operated,'' not to modeling paradigms in the abstract.

Fourth, the large-scale state of JFM 2026 conditions the attainable
predictability. \ph{Describe the concurrent ENSO state, MJO activity,
and any stratospheric events during JFM 2026, citing monitoring
sources, and discuss qualitatively how these may have opened or
closed windows of forecast opportunity}
\citep{mariotti2020,domeisen2022,wheelerhendon2004,zhang2005}.

Finally, the operational context matters for India specifically:
winter precipitation governs rabi-season agriculture, Himalayan
snowpack, and water resources, and India's operational extended-range
activities provide a natural consumer for such benchmarks
\citep{rao2019,pattanaik2019,dimri2015,hunt2018}. \ph{Discuss

% LIMITATIONS

\section{Limitations}\label{sec:limitations}

Several limitations qualify the conclusions. The evaluation covers a
single season with $T=13$ initializations, so sampling uncertainty is
large, score differences are often statistically indistinguishable,
and the results characterize JFM 2026 rather than climatological
skill. ERA5 is treated as truth, yet its precipitation is a model
product with documented deficiencies over complex terrain, including
the Himalaya and orographic northwest India, and verification against
gauge-based analyses can alter both magnitudes and rankings
\citep{hersbach2020,pai2014}. Only ensemble means are verified, with
unequal ensemble sizes across systems, which advantages larger
ensembles at long leads and ignores probabilistic information
entirely. The anomaly-referencing strategy is heterogeneous --- model
climatologies derive from reforecast sets of differing length and
configuration, and any in-sample correction applied where reforecasts
are unavailable optimistically biases the corrected system
(Section~\ref{sec:anom}). Operational systems may have undergone
configuration changes within or shortly before the verification
period, so the systems evaluated are snapshots. Deterministic metrics
on weekly means mask timing errors within the week and say nothing
about sharpness or reliability. Finally, AI systems trained on ERA5
and initialized from (or near) ERA5 analyses enjoy a structural
consistency advantage when verified against ERA5 itself, and
training-period overlap with the verification season cannot be fully
excluded for recently trained systems; these caveats apply with
particular force to FuXi-S2S and Spire AI-S2S
(Section~\ref{sec:models}).

\section{Future Work}\label{sec:future}

Natural extensions include: (i)~adding further systems as access is
secured, including FourCastNet-3 \ph{[CITE]}, the Deep Learning Earth
System Model \citep{cresswellclay2024}, and the IMD Extended Range
Forecast System \citep{pattanaik2019}, the last enabling a direct
operational comparison for India; (ii)~extending the variable set to
2-m temperature and 500-hPa geopotential height, for which weekly
predictability horizons differ markedly from precipitation;
(iii)~extending verification to weeks 5--6 where forecast ranges
permit; (iv)~probabilistic verification (CRPS, RPSS, reliability
diagrams, ROC) using full ensembles rather than means; (v)~tercile
categorical verification (above/near/below normal) with Heidke and
Gerrity scores, aligning with Indian operational convention
\citep{pattanaik2019,wilks2019}; (vi)~stratification of skill by MJO
phase \citep{wheelerhendon2004} and by the concurrent ENSO state to
identify windows of opportunity \citep{mariotti2020};
(vii)~multi-winter extension as real-time AI archives lengthen, and
companion hindcast verification where reforecasts exist;
(viii)~cross-verification against IMD gauge-based gridded rainfall
and satellite products such as IMERG to test sensitivity to the
choice of truth \citep{pai2014}; and (ix)~process-oriented
evaluation, in particular objective tracking of western disturbances
in forecasts and reanalysis \citep{hunt2018,dimri2015}.

spanning JFM 2026, verifying ensemble-mean anomalies against ERA5
